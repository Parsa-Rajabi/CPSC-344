## Why We Are Taking This Approach

CPSC 344 is a user-centred design course. Since students are the people most directly using AI tools for learning in this course, this policy is informed by student input from the **Week 2B class activity**. In that activity, participating students identified common acceptable uses, such as brainstorming, grammar support, study support, and idea refinement, as well as unacceptable uses, such as AI use during exams, copying AI output directly, using AI for entire assignments, and using AI for peer evaluations.

This also reflects the HCI process we are learning in the course. We are treating this policy as something designed for a specific context:

1. understand the users and context,
2. design a policy and disclosure process,
3. implement it in the course,
4. evaluate how well it works,
5. revise it based on evidence and feedback.

The teaching team will gather feedback on this policy between Term 1 and Term 2, and near the beginning of Term 2. We will use that feedback to understand what is working, what is unclear, and what may need to change.

By remaining enrolled in CPSC 344, you are responsible for following this policy and any future revisions announced by the teaching team.

## Relationship to UBC, Faculty, and Department Policies

This course policy operates under all relevant UBC, Faculty of Science, Department of Computer Science, academic integrity, privacy, copyright, and research ethics policies.

If any part of this course policy conflicts with a higher-level UBC, Faculty, Department, legal, privacy, copyright, or research ethics requirement, the higher-level requirement applies.

This course policy is meant to fill in course-specific gaps and operationalize AI use for CPSC 344 within reason. It does not replace university policy.

[UBC Academic Integrity guidance on Generative AI](https://academicintegrity.ubc.ca/genai/) states that whether generative AI is allowed in a course is a course- or program-level decision, and that instructors should clarify expectations and permitted limits. It also notes that if AI use is prohibited or exceeds the instructor's stated limits, it may be treated as academic misconduct.

## General Principle

AI may support learning, brainstorming, editing, studying, prototyping, and productivity, but it must not replace your own thinking, design judgment, authorship, contribution, or accountability.

A useful guiding question is:

> Did AI help me do the work, or did AI do the work for me?

AI use is more likely to be acceptable when it helps you understand, brainstorm, revise, or check your work. AI use is not acceptable when it replaces the learning, reasoning, design work, analysis, evaluation, or contribution that the course is asking you to demonstrate.

---

## Exams

The midterm and final exam will be paper-based.

Students will not have electronic access during the exams. AI tools are not permitted during the midterm or final exam.

Using AI during an exam is not allowed.

---

## Take-Home Assessments

AI may be used for take-home assessments only when it is disclosed using the course disclosure process and only when the use is consistent with this policy.

This applies to take-home work such as assignments, project submissions, reports, presentations, prototype descriptions, design materials, and other submitted course materials.

If AI contributed to the submitted work, you must disclose it. This includes cases where AI helped with wording, structure, design ideas, diagrams, summaries, research directions, critique, or other parts of the work.

You remain responsible for the accuracy, quality, ethics, and originality of the final submission. You are also responsible for being able to explain and defend what you submit.

Private studying with AI does not need to be disclosed unless it directly contributes to submitted work. AI is still not permitted during exams.

For optional AI literacy material, see the [AI Learning Support section of Extra Resources](extra-resources.md#ai-learning-support).

## Acceptable AI Uses

The following uses are generally acceptable when they are disclosed and when the final work remains your own or your group's responsibility.

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| Brainstorming early ideas | Asking AI to suggest possible design directions before your group chooses and develops its own approach |
| Clarifying course concepts | Asking AI to explain design requirements, cognitive walkthroughs, task analysis, or usability testing in simpler terms |
| Generating study questions | Asking AI to create practice questions for review |
| Grammar and language editing | Asking AI to identify unclear wording or sentence-level issues |
| Formatting support | Asking AI to help organize student-written notes into headings or tables |
| Summarizing your own notes | Asking AI to summarize your own meeting notes, provided they do not contain participant data or restricted course materials |
| Creating diagrams or flow charts | Asking AI to help draft a process diagram, concept map, or flow chart when diagrams are allowed for the assignment |
| Critiquing student-generated ideas | Asking AI to identify possible weaknesses in a design idea your group created |
| Research direction support | Asking AI for possible search terms, concepts, or areas to investigate, while verifying all sources yourself |
| Prototype planning support | Asking AI to help think through prototype scope, interaction flow, trade-offs, or possible evaluation questions |

These examples are not a blank cheque. The important question is whether the submitted work still reflects your own or your group's understanding, decision-making, and contribution.

## Unacceptable AI Uses

The following uses are not acceptable in CPSC 344.

| Unacceptable Use | Why It Is Not Allowed |
|---|---|
| Using AI during exams | Exams are individual, paper-based, no-electronic-access assessments |
| Copying and pasting AI output without meaningful review or disclosure | This misrepresents authorship and process |
| Generating an entire assignment, report, presentation, or project submission | This replaces the work being assessed |
| Using AI to replace your own analysis, design reasoning, or evaluation work | CPSC 344 assesses your ability to reason through HCI methods |
| Fabricating sources, citations, data, participant responses, or findings | This is a serious academic integrity concern |
| Submitting work you cannot explain | Students and groups must be able to explain and defend their submissions |
| Using AI for peer evaluation ratings or comments | Peer evaluation must reflect your own judgment and professional responsibility |
| Using AI in ways that misrepresent individual or group contribution | Group work depends on honest contribution and accountability |
| Uploading lecture notes or workshop notes into AI tools | Course materials are protected by copyright and may include material not licensed for third-party AI systems |
| Uploading participant data into AI tools | This is strictly prohibited and may trigger research ethics consequences |
| Using AI plagiarism or AI-detection tools on student work | These tools are not permitted in CPSC 344 Section 901, Summer 2026 |

## Participant Data and AI Tools

> [!WARNING]
> **Do not input participant data into any AI tool. This is strictly not allowed.**

This is the most serious restriction in this policy.

CPSC 344 projects involve HCI methods such as observation, interview, and questionnaire work. Because of this, students may handle sensitive project data even in a course context.

You may **not** upload, paste, summarize, transform, analyze, or ask an AI tool to work with any participant data. This includes, but is not limited to:

- interview transcripts,
- interview notes,
- observation notes,
- questionnaire responses,
- raw survey data,
- exported survey results,
- audio or video recordings,
- participant quotes,
- screenshots involving participants,
- identifiable participant information,
- de-identified participant information,
- coded participant data,
- themes or summaries generated directly from participant data,
- any data collected from people as part of the course project.

This applies even if the AI tool claims that it does not train on your data. This applies even if the data has been anonymized or de-identified.

[TCPS 2, Chapter 5](https://ethics.gc.ca/eng/tcps2-eptc2_2022_chapter5-chapitre5.html) states that researchers have an ethical duty of confidentiality and must safeguard participant information. It describes safeguards as including physical, administrative, and technical measures to protect data from unauthorized access, loss, or modification. [UBC Behavioural Research Ethics guidance](https://researchethics.ubc.ca/about-behavioural-research) also states that UBC's Behavioural Research Ethics Board reviews behavioural and social science research, including research using interviews, focus groups, surveys, questionnaires, and observations. [UBC Privacy Matters guidance for AI tools](https://privacymatters.ubc.ca/sites/default/files/2025-10/PIA_Reviewed_AI_2025-09.pdf) states that AI tools that collect, store, or process personal information must be evaluated for compliance with BC FIPPA and UBC Information Security Standards.

If a student or group inputs participant data into an AI tool, the matter will be referred to the appropriate university research ethics office, including UBC BREB / Office of Research Ethics, for review. This may also be treated as a serious academic integrity and research ethics concern.

<details>
<summary><strong>What should I do instead?</strong></summary>

Use the approved course process for handling participant data.

You may use AI to help design your interview questions, refine your observation protocol, brainstorm possible questionnaire wording, or think through analysis plans **before** data is collected, as long as no participant data is entered into the AI tool.

Once you have collected participant data, do not place that data into an AI tool. If you are unsure what counts as participant data, ask the teaching team before using the tool.

</details>

## AI Plagiarism Checkers and AI Detectors

> [!IMPORTANT]
> AI plagiarism checkers and AI-detection tools are **not permitted** in CPSC 344 Section 901, Summer 2026.

Students, group members, TAs, and the teaching team must not input student work, peer work, group submissions, or any student data into AI plagiarism checkers or AI-detection tools.

These tools are not welcomed in this course. [UBC Academic Integrity guidance](https://academicintegrity.ubc.ca/genai/) strongly discourages the use of AI-detection applications because of concerns about effectiveness, accuracy, bias, privacy, and intellectual property.

If there is a concern about AI use, the teaching team will rely on disclosure review, student explanation, process evidence, assignment context, and academic integrity procedures, not AI detector scores.

## Course Materials, Lecture Notes, Workshop Notes, and Copyright

> [!WARNING]
> Do not upload CPSC 344 lecture notes, workshop notes, or slides into AI tools unless the instructor has explicitly given permission.

Course materials are protected by copyright. [UBC Copyright guidance](https://copyright.ubc.ca/copyright-guidelines/) notes that copyright owners have the right to control reproduction, publication, adaptation, translation, and communication of their work. [UBC's Teaching Materials Policy](https://universitycounsel.ubc.ca/policies/teaching-materials-policy/) describes rights and responsibilities around teaching materials created at or under the auspices of UBC. [The Canadian Copyright Act](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/C-42/section-3.html) similarly gives copyright owners the sole right to produce or reproduce a work, or any substantial part of it.

Uploading course materials into a third-party AI tool may involve copying, transmitting, or making those materials available to a system outside the course context. For this reason, students may not input CPSC 344 lecture notes, workshop notes, slides, handouts, or similar course materials into AI tools.

If you want to use AI to study from course materials, contact **Parsa at cpsc344-admin@cs.ubc.ca**. He can provide support, which may include developing course-specific chatbots or agents that have appropriate access to course materials. This keeps the copyright owner in charge of how much course content is shared with any third-party system.

<details>
<summary><strong>What is allowed?</strong></summary>

You may ask AI general questions about HCI concepts, such as:

- “Explain cognitive walkthroughs in simple terms.”
- “What is the difference between a design constraint and a design goal?”
- “Give me practice questions about usability testing.”

You may not paste CPSC 344 lecture slides, workshop notes, or assignment materials into the tool unless permission has been given.

</details>

## AI Disclosure Requirement

When AI is used for take-home assessments, students must submit an AI disclosure.

There are three ways to do this:

1. use the copy/paste AI disclosure prompt after an AI session,
2. use the multi-tool combination prompt if multiple AI tools or sessions were used,
3. complete the manual AI disclosure template without using AI.

The most important part is honest disclosure, not perfect wording.

The disclosure should make clear:

- what tool was used,
- what it was used for,
- what AI contributed,
- what the student or group changed, verified, rejected, or added,
- whether AI-generated or AI-influenced material appears in the final submission,
- that the student or group remains responsible for the submitted work.

---

## Group Disclosure Requirement

For group submissions, the AI disclosure is considered complete only when **all group members have submitted their individual AI disclosure**.

If even one group member's disclosure is missing, the group disclosure is considered **incomplete/missing**.

It is strongly recommended that one person on the team do a final sanity check before submitting the assignment to make sure all group members have submitted their disclosures.

This does not mean one person is responsible for everyone else's AI use. It means the group should have a clear internal process so the final submission is complete.

A useful group workflow is:

1. each group member completes their own AI disclosure,
2. one person checks that all disclosures are submitted,
3. the group submits the assignment only after the disclosure set is complete.

---

## Incomplete/Missing AI Disclosure Penalties

If a required AI disclosure is incomplete or missing, the following penalty structure applies.

| Instance | Consequence |
|---:|---|
| First instance | The group receives an official warning from the teaching team |
| Second instance | The group receives a **-7% penalty** on the specific submission |
| Third instance | The group receives a **-15% penalty** on the specific submission |
| Fourth and later instances | The group receives a **-25% penalty** on the specific submission |

These penalties apply to the specific submission where the disclosure issue occurred.

For individual submissions, the consequence applies to the individual student's submission.

For group submissions, the consequence normally applies to the group submission. The teaching team will not normally apply the course AI disclosure penalty to only one member of a group submission.

---

## Disclosure Correction Window

When a disclosure is incomplete or missing, the group has **2 business days / 48 hours** from notification to produce the missing or incomplete disclosure.

If the group submits the completed disclosure within that window, the case will be handled according to the relevant instance level above. For example, a first incomplete/missing disclosure would normally result in an official warning.

If the group does not submit the completed disclosure by the deadline:

1. the case is treated as a second instance,
2. a **-7% penalty** is applied to the specific submission,
3. the 48-hour timer resets,
4. if the disclosure is still not completed, the penalty level continues to increase according to the incomplete/missing disclosure penalty structure.

This correction window is meant to support transparency. It is not meant to make disclosure optional.

## Group Work and Internal Group Agreements

Project groups are strongly encouraged to discuss AI use within their own teams.

Your group should decide, early and explicitly:

- which AI uses are acceptable within your team,
- how AI use will be documented,
- who is responsible for preparing and submitting disclosures,
- who will do the final disclosure sanity check,
- how teammates will communicate about AI use,
- what happens internally if a group member uses AI in a way that violates the group's agreement or the course policy.

The teaching team will not normally apply the course AI disclosure penalty to only one member of a group submission. If the issue appears in the group's submitted work, it will normally be treated as an issue with the group submission.

This means your group contract or internal team agreement should be detailed enough to handle AI-related expectations and responsibilities within the group.

A useful group norm might be:

> Before adding AI-influenced material to a group submission, tell the group what tool was used, what it contributed, and what still needs to be checked or revised.

## Audits of AI Disclosure Forms and Student Work

To support fairness and academic integrity, the teaching team may conduct audits of AI disclosure forms and submitted work throughout the semester.

Audits may include:

- reviewing submitted AI disclosure forms,
- comparing the disclosure with the submitted work,
- asking students or groups to explain how AI was used,
- scheduled conversations or interviews about AI use,
- asking students to explain design decisions, written claims, prototype choices, diagrams, user scenarios, design requirements, evaluation plans, or project contributions.

If inconsistencies are found, students may be asked to provide clarification.

Persistent discrepancies, misleading disclosures, or inability to explain submitted work may lead to further review for possible academic integrity concerns.

The purpose of these audits is not to punish students for using AI. The purpose is to make sure AI use is transparent, accurately disclosed, and consistent with the learning goals of the course.

---

## Choosing Not to Use AI

You are not required to use AI tools in CPSC 344.

If you do not wish to use AI tools, that is a valid decision.

AI use in education can be messy and unpredictable. Some students may have ethical concerns, privacy concerns, environmental concerns, accessibility concerns, or uncertainty about how AI tools affect learning. This policy is not meant to promote AI use. It is meant to anticipate and reduce potential harms when AI tools are used.

Choosing not to use AI will not put you at a disadvantage in the course. The assessments are designed so that AI is not required.
