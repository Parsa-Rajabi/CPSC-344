# Group Work Resources

Group work is a major part of CPSC 344. These resources are meant to help your team turn the group contract into practical habits, not just a document you submit once.

## Start Here

If you are short on time or feeling overloaded, focus on these first:

1. Agree on weekly meeting time, response time, and where tasks are tracked.
2. Assign project roles with an owner and backup.
3. Decide how the team will make decisions when people disagree.
4. Write down what happens when someone misses a meeting, deadline, or task.
5. Revisit the contract before Milestone 2 and Milestone 4 peer evaluations.

Your group contract should describe how your team will actually work. Avoid vague answers like "we will communicate well" or "we will divide work fairly." A useful contract names specific behaviours, timelines, tools, roles, and consequences.

<details open>
<summary><strong>Preparing for the Contract Meeting</strong></summary>

Before your contract meeting, each team member should individually prepare answers to these questions:

- What times am I usually unavailable?
- What kinds of project work am I strongest at?
- What kinds of project work do I want to learn or practise?
- What communication habits help me work well?
- What communication habits frustrate me?
- What does "equal contribution" mean for this project?
- What should happen if someone misses a meeting, deadline, or task?

During the meeting, do not rush to fill blanks. Discuss the answers first, then write the agreement.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Group Values and Roles</strong></summary>

Good answers name how the team wants to work and who owns recurring coordination tasks.

Weak answer:

> We value respect and everyone will help.

Stronger answer:

> We value reliability, direct communication, and evidence-based design decisions. Reliability means attending scheduled meetings or giving at least 24 hours notice when possible. Direct communication means raising blockers early rather than waiting until the deadline. Evidence-based design means connecting major design choices to research findings, TA feedback, peer critique, or prototype testing.

For secondary roles, separate responsibility from authority. Every group member is a designer and researcher. A role means someone is responsible for making sure a kind of work is visible, coordinated, and completed. It does **not** mean that person makes decisions alone or does all the work in that area.

Your contract should assign a small set of standing roles for the full term and milestone roles that rotate as the project changes. Each role should have an owner and a backup so the team is not blocked if one person is unavailable.

### Standing Roles

These roles should exist throughout the project.

| Role | Main responsibility | Good evidence the role is being done |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Project Coordinator | Maintains the project timeline, tracks tasks, checks progress before internal deadlines, and flags risks early. | A visible task list with owners, due dates, and current status. |
| TA Liaison | Coordinates questions for the TA or instructor and brings guidance back to the team. | Questions are collected before co-working sessions, and answers are summarized for the group. |
| Meeting Facilitator | Prepares agendas, keeps meetings focused, and makes sure decisions are named before moving on. | Meetings end with decisions, action items, owners, and deadlines. |
| Decision Log Steward | Maintains the team's design decision log across milestones. | Major changes and unaccepted TA suggestions are recorded with reasons and evidence. |
| File and Submission Manager | Keeps shared files organized, confirms link permissions, and checks that the submitted version is correct. | The final files are findable, accessible to the teaching team, and submitted on time. |

### Milestone Roles

These roles should rotate or be reassigned as the project moves from need-finding to design, prototyping, and evaluation. A team member can hold more than one role, but avoid putting all high-responsibility roles on the same person.

| Role | Most relevant stages | Main responsibility |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Research Operations Lead | Milestone 1, Milestone 2 | Coordinates research questions, participant access, study materials, scheduling, and ethical handling of data. |
| Evidence and Synthesis Lead | Milestone 2, Milestone 4 | Makes sure findings, themes, requirements, and recommendations are traceable to actual evidence. |
| Requirements and Metrics Lead | Milestone 2, Checkpoint A, Checkpoint B | Keeps design requirements, central tasks, and metrics consistent across deliverables. |
| Design Exploration Lead | Milestone 3 | Coordinates brainstorming, reference design critique, alternative concepts, and low-fidelity sketches. |
| Prototype Scope Lead | Checkpoint A, Milestone 4 | Defines what must be functional, simulated, polished, simplified, or omitted for the prototype. |
| Evaluation Lead | Checkpoint B, Milestone 4 | Coordinates evaluation research questions, study procedure, materials, data collection, and analysis plan. |
| Writing Integration Lead | Milestone 2, Milestone 4 | Makes the report read like one coherent argument instead of separate stitched-together sections. |
| Presentation and Demo Lead | Milestone 1, Milestone 3, Demo Showcase | Coordinates slide flow, speaking order, demo plan, timing, and Q&A preparation. |
| Quality and Rubric Reviewer | Every deliverable | Checks the work against the milestone instructions, rubric, formatting expectations, and missing evidence. |

### Role Assignment Template

Use a table like this in your contract or team notes:

| Role | Owner | Backup | What this person checks | When they check it |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Project Coordinator | | | Task list, deadlines, blockers | Twice per week |
| TA Liaison | | | TA questions and answers | Before and after co-working |
| Decision Log Steward | | | Major design decisions and unaccepted TA suggestions | After each feedback session |
| Quality and Rubric Reviewer | | | Rubric fit, missing evidence, submission details | 48 hours before submission |

### Choosing Roles Well

Do not assign roles only based on who is already good at something. Use roles to balance learning and reliability.

Good role assignments consider:

- skill: who can do this well?
- growth: who wants to practise this?
- availability: who can realistically own this during the relevant week?
- workload: who is already carrying heavy tasks?
- continuity: who understands the previous decision or artifact?

Roles should be revisited at each milestone. At minimum, check whether the role assignments still make sense after Milestone 1, after Milestone 2, after Shark Tank, and before Milestone 4.

### What Roles Should Not Do

Roles should not create hierarchy or hide unequal work.

Avoid these patterns:

- The Project Coordinator becomes the team's boss.
- The TA Liaison is the only person allowed to understand TA feedback.
- The strongest writer rewrites everyone's work at the last minute.
- The prototype lead builds everything alone.
- The research lead collects data without the team understanding the protocol.
- The quality reviewer is blamed for problems that should have been addressed earlier.

When a role owner notices a problem, their job is to make the problem visible early and help the team decide what to do.

<details>
<summary><strong>Communication and Decision Making</strong></summary>

Your contract should make communication predictable.

Include:

- primary channel for normal updates
- backup channel for urgent issues
- expected response time
- meeting schedule
- how meeting agendas are created
- where action items are stored
- how decisions are documented

Example:

> We will use Slack for normal communication and text message only for urgent same-day issues. Team members will respond to Slack messages within 24 hours on weekdays and within 36 hours on weekends. We will meet every Tuesday from 5:00-6:00pm and use a shared Google Doc for agendas and action items. The note taker will record decisions, owners, and due dates before the meeting ends.

Teams often get stuck because they have not agreed on how decisions happen. Your contract should define both the preferred process and the fallback process.

Decision-making example:

> We will try to make major decisions by consensus after discussing options and tradeoffs. If we cannot reach consensus after 20 minutes, we will list the options, identify the criteria that matter, and vote. If the vote is tied, we will ask our TA for advice during co-working hours or choose the option that best fits the milestone rubric and available evidence.

For design decisions, record:

- what options were considered
- what evidence or feedback mattered
- what decision was made
- what the team will revisit later

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Work Quality, AI Use, and Accountability</strong></summary>

Be specific about quality standards for each kind of work.

Example:

> Draft writing should be complete enough for teammates to edit for argument and evidence, not just grammar. Slides should have speaker notes or a clear explanation of what the presenter will say. Research notes should include date, method, participant label, and which research question the notes relate to. No one submits their own section without at least one teammate reviewing it.

Useful review routine:

1. Owner completes a draft.
2. Reviewer checks against the rubric and project goals.
3. Owner revises.
4. Final reviewer checks formatting, citations, links, and submission requirements.

If your team uses AI tools, agree on boundaries before the work starts.

Your contract should answer:

- Which tools are allowed?
- Which tasks are AI tools allowed to support?
- Which tasks must remain human-authored or human-verified?
- How will AI use be disclosed to teammates?
- Who checks accuracy, citations, privacy, and course policy compliance?

Example:

> AI tools may be used for brainstorming alternatives, improving sentence clarity, and generating checklists. AI tools may not be used to invent research findings, fabricate participant data, replace team discussion, or produce final claims without human verification. Any AI-assisted text must be reviewed by at least one teammate for accuracy, tone, and fit with our actual evidence.

Define contribution in visible, checkable terms.

Useful categories:

- attending meetings and workshops
- preparing before meetings
- completing assigned tasks by agreed deadlines
- communicating blockers early
- reviewing teammates' work
- contributing to presentations and demos
- participating in research, prototyping, and evaluation work

Example:

> Each member is expected to attend weekly team meetings, attend workshops, complete assigned tasks by the agreed internal deadline, and communicate blockers at least 24 hours before the internal deadline when possible. If someone cannot complete a task, they must tell the team early enough for the task to be reassigned or resized.

## Conflict Resolution

Conflict is not automatically a sign that the team is failing. It often means expectations, priorities, or constraints are not aligned yet. The goal is to address the issue early, before it becomes a peer evaluation problem or a milestone crisis.

This section is inspired by the Program on Negotiation at Harvard Law School article [5 Conflict Resolution Strategies That Actually Work](https://www.pon.harvard.edu/daily/conflict-resolution/conflict-resolution-strategies/).

Use this process before contacting the teaching team, unless the issue involves safety, harassment, discrimination, academic misconduct, or another serious concern that needs immediate support.

<div class="accordion">

<details open>
<summary><strong>Step-by-Step Conflict Process</strong></summary>

### Step 1: Pause and Name the Issue

Focus on the behaviour or decision, not the person's character.

Useful wording:

> I think we are stuck on how to divide the prototype work. Can we pause and agree on what needs to be done, who owns each piece, and what deadline is realistic?

Avoid:

> You never help.

Use:

> The last two assigned tasks were not finished by the internal deadline, and the team had to redistribute them the night before submission.

### Step 2: Check Perspectives

People often disagree because they are using different definitions of fairness, quality, or urgency. Ask each person to explain how they see the situation.

Useful questions:

- What feels unfair or unclear here?
- What constraint are we not accounting for?
- What do you need from the team to complete your part?
- What does a fair outcome look like to you?
- What evidence, rubric requirement, or deadline should guide this decision?

### Step 3: Look for Shared Goals

Shift from "my side versus your side" to the shared project outcome.

Useful framing:

> We all need the Milestone 2 report to be coherent, evidence-based, and submitted on time. What division of work gets us there without overloading one person?

Shared goals might include:

- submitting on time
- producing work the team can defend
- keeping workload visible
- making sure everyone can explain the project
- protecting the team's peer evaluation process
- maintaining a respectful working relationship

### Step 4: Identify the Underlying Concern

Many conflicts are not really about the first issue named. They may be about trust, workload, unclear standards, lack of recognition, or fear that the project is going in the wrong direction.

Ask:

- What are you most worried will happen?
- What would make this feel workable?
- What would make this feel fair?
- What information do we need before deciding?

### Step 5: Agree on a Specific Next Action

End the conversation with concrete next steps.

Record:

- decision made
- task owner
- deadline
- expected output
- review plan
- what happens if the task is blocked again

Example:

> Alex will draft the observation findings by Tuesday at 6:00pm. Priya will review by Wednesday at noon. If Alex cannot finish by Tuesday at 6:00pm, they will message the group by Monday at 6:00pm so the task can be split.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Common Problems and What to Do</strong></summary>

| Problem | First team action | If it continues |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Missed meeting | Ask what happened and confirm whether the meeting time still works. Record missed action items. | Revisit attendance expectations and consequences in the contract. |
| Late task | Ask for a status update, blocker, and revised deadline. Resize or reassign if needed. | Document the pattern and discuss it in a team meeting. |
| Uneven workload | List all tasks completed and upcoming tasks. Rebalance visible work. | Use the contract consequences and prepare documentation for TA support if needed. |
| Low-quality work | Define the quality gap using the rubric or team standard. Assign a revision owner and reviewer. | Create a review checklist and require earlier drafts. |
| Unresponsive teammate | Use the agreed backup channel. Ask for a specific reply by a specific time. | Document contact attempts and contact the TA if the issue affects progress. |
| Decision deadlock | Name the decision criteria, compare options, and use the contract fallback process. | Ask the TA for guidance with options and tradeoffs prepared. |
| Tension after feedback | Separate the feedback from the person. Decide what action the team will take. | Ask the TA to facilitate if the team cannot discuss the issue productively. |

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Escalating to the Teaching Team</strong></summary>

The syllabus expects teams to try peer resolution first. If internal resolution does not work, contact your TA early. Do not wait until the peer evaluation deadline.

Before contacting the TA, prepare:

- the relevant contract section
- dates of missed meetings, missed deadlines, or unresolved issues
- meeting notes or messages showing attempts to resolve the issue
- what the team has already tried
- what decision or support you need from the TA

Useful message template:

> Hi [TA name], our group has tried to resolve a project participation issue internally, but it is still affecting our progress. We have documented the issue and our attempted resolution steps. Could we schedule a meeting during co-working hours or another available time? We are looking for guidance on [specific issue].

## Retrospectives and Peer Evaluation

<div class="accordion">

<details open>
<summary><strong>Team Retrospectives</strong></summary>

Use a short retrospective before Milestone 2 and Milestone 4 peer evaluations, and any time the team feels stuck.

Suggested agenda:

1. What is working well?
2. What is not working well?
3. What should we start doing?
4. What should we stop doing?
5. What should we continue doing?
6. Are our contract expectations still realistic?
7. What is one concrete change we will make before the next milestone?

Keep the discussion behaviour-focused. Record action items and owners.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Peer Evaluation Notes</strong></summary>

Peer evaluations should be professional, specific, and behaviour-based. They should not be used as a surprise punishment for concerns that were never raised.

Useful comment pattern:

> [Name] contributed by [specific behaviour or task]. One area for improvement is [specific behaviour]. For example, [specific instance]. This affected the team by [impact].

Examples:

> Jordan consistently prepared meeting agendas and kept the team on track. For Milestone 2, they also reviewed the task analysis section and caught missing links to the research questions.

> Sam completed their assigned slides, but the drafts were often submitted after the internal deadline. This made it harder for the team to review the presentation as a whole. A helpful next step would be to send a partial draft earlier if the final version is delayed.

Avoid comments about personality, popularity, or assumptions about intent.

</details>

## Quick Checklists

<div class="accordion">

<details>
<summary><strong>Before Each Meeting</strong></summary>

- Is there an agenda?
- Does each agenda item have a purpose?
- Are blockers listed?
- Are decisions needed?
- Are files or drafts linked?

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>During Each Meeting</strong></summary>

- Confirm what changed since the last meeting.
- Make decisions explicit.
- Assign owners and deadlines.
- Record unresolved questions.
- Confirm the next meeting time.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Before Each Submission</strong></summary>

- Has every member reviewed the final deliverable?
- Can every member explain the core project decisions?
- Are links accessible to the teaching team?
- Does the submission match the milestone instructions?
- Are contributions and remaining concerns documented?

</details>
